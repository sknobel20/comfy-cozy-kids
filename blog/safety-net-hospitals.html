What "safety-net hospital" actually means, and why it matters for kids

This past summer, I spent my civic learning internship at Tufts University researching how pediatric care access is distributed across Boston neighborhoods. While I expected to see inequalities, I didn't expect the numbers to be so stark.

The term itself

A safety-net hospital is one that treats a disproportionate share of low-income, uninsured, or underinsured patients, regardless of their ability to pay. In Boston, hospitals like Boston Medical Center and Franciscan Children's fill that role. They tend to operate on thinner margins and receive far less in private donations and endowment funding than academic medical centers like Boston Children's Hospital, even though they're often the only realistic option for families in their neighborhoods.

Where you live matters

Access to child healthcare should mean consistent, affordable, timely care: a regular pediatrician, vaccinations, dental and vision care, mental health support, and specialty care when it's needed. For a lot of kids in Boston, that's not the reality. Lack of access looks like no regular pediatrician, months-long waits, delayed treatment for chronic illness, and unmet mental health needs, often compounded by language barriers and gaps in insurance coverage.

In 2024, East Boston had 9,788 children and 1 primary care practice. Back Bay, by comparison, had 2,139 children and 15 primary care practices. That's not a small gap, it's the difference between a neighborhood with practically no local pediatric access to most and one with more practices than it has thousands of kids to serve.

That divide isn't random. East Boston is majority Latino and Hispanic while Back Bay is majority White. Citywide, majority-POC neighborhoods like Mattapan, Dorchester, and Roxbury tend to have fewer pediatric clinics, longer wait times, and heavier reliance on MassHealth, alongside higher rates of unmet health needs and preventable ER visits. These patterns trace back to decades of residential segregation and underinvestment.

It's not just geography

Race is not the only major factor. Girls and LGBTQ+ youth face higher mental health needs alongside longer delays in getting care, and female caregivers often absorb the time, cost, and logistical burden of navigating the system. Socioeconomic status is another major factor: families relying on MassHealth are more likely to face clinic shortages, long waits, and transportation barriers that delay or prevent care altogether.

Why this shapes our work

This is why BLS Comfy Cozy Kids focuses on hospitals like Boston Medical Center and Franciscan Children's rather than hospitals that already have strong donor bases. BMC's Kids Fund and Pediatric Outreach program serves many of the same low-income and immigrant families from Roxbury, Dorchester, and the South End that show up in this data, offering free primary care, dental care, screenings, and behavioral health services, alongside housing and nutrition support that shapes children's health long before they ever see a doctor.

Our card drives and fundraising aren't going to close a gap this large on their own. But they are a way of showing up for hospitals and families that too often get overlooked, and a reminder that the kids on the other end of a card or a donation deserve the same healthcare that kids a few neighborhoods over already have.
